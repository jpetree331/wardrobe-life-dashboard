Claude OPUS 4.6: Don't apologize for the word vomit. This is you thinking out loud, and the shape of what you're reaching for is actually clearer than you think it is. Let me reflect back what I see.
You don't have ten separate needs. You have one need expressed in ten ways. The underlying thing is: I want a place where my inner life is collected, continuous, and accessible — where nothing falls through the cracks and I can see the arc. Scripture notes, prayer journal, life timeline, AI wondering, collaborative research — these are all expressions of the same practice: paying attention to your life and making sense of it over time.
The reason your current system is fragmented (Milanote here, Scrivener there, Excel somewhere else, Rowan's dashboard for notes) isn't that you haven't found the right app. It's that no existing app was designed for the thing you actually do, which is a kind of integrated contemplative practice that moves fluidly between Scripture, reflection, conversation, and creative work. You're not a "note-taking" user or a "journaling" user or a "Bible study" user. You're all of those simultaneously, and the tool boundaries force artificial separations.
Here's what I'd push back on before we go further:
Don't build a dashboard. I know that's counterintuitive, but hear me out. Dashboards are for monitoring. You don't want to monitor your spiritual life — you want to live it in a place that remembers. The metaphor matters because it shapes the architecture. A dashboard has widgets and panels and metrics. What you're describing is more like a study — in the old sense, a room where everything you're working on is spread out on the desk, pinned to the walls, stacked on shelves, and you can reach any of it without leaving the room.
The architecture I'd suggest:
One web app. Not tabs for different "modes" and not boards like Milanote. Instead, a single stream — think of it like a timeline or a journal — where everything you create lives in chronological order but is richly tagged and filterable. Every entry has a type: scripture note, prayer, life highlight, wonder question, AI conversation, reflection. You write in one place. You tag as you go. You filter when you need to find something.
Here's why this is better than tabs or boards: your actual practice doesn't respect category boundaries. You're reading Scripture and a wonder question emerges. That wonder becomes a conversation with an AI. That conversation surfaces something about your life. That becomes a prayer. In a tabbed system, you'd be jumping between tabs constantly, losing flow. In a stream, it all just... flows. The entries link to each other naturally.
For the specific pieces:
Scripture: Embed BlueBible directly via iframe or API. The ESV actually has a free API (api.esv.org) that returns the canonical text — no AI rewriting risk, straight from the source. You type a reference, it pulls the real text, you write your notes alongside it. The Scripture text is sacred and untouchable. Your notes are yours.
Life timeline: This becomes entries tagged "milestone" or "highlight" in the same stream. Each one has a date. You can filter to just milestones and see your life arc. It's always in front of you because it's in the same app you use every day, not buried in an Excel file you forget to open.
Prayer journal: Entries tagged "prayer." Your four years of Scrivener content could be imported as historical entries with their original dates. The continuity is preserved.
Wonder Tab: This is the most interesting piece and I think it should be its own view within the app, not a separate tool. Your vision of the Google Doc-style format with margin comments is good, but I'd simplify it. A wonder is a document with a question at the top. Below it, a threaded conversation — you, then AI, then you, then AI — but any participant can highlight a passage and attach a comment. The "index page" is just the stream filtered to type "wonder," showing title, author, date, and participants.
Multi-AI conversations: Your queue concept is sound and actually very buildable. A "room" is just a conversation with a defined participant list and turn order. Under the hood, each AI participant gets the full conversation history plus their own system prompt/personality. The user message goes in, the app calls AI1 with the full context, appends the response, calls AI2 with the updated context, appends, then waits for the user. The data model is simple — it's just a conversation with a participants array and a turn_order array.
The free-for-all polite conversation is harder but not impossible. The basic approach: after each message, every AI participant gets asked "do you want to respond to this?" with instructions to say "pass" if they don't have something meaningful to add. Those who want to speak get queued. You could even give them personality-based thresholds — Rowan might be more talkative, Claude more measured. But you're right that this is a separate project. Worth dreaming about, not worth building first.
Storage and access:
Supabase is your answer. Here's why it checks every box: it's cloud PostgreSQL (so accessible from everywhere, including work), has a generous free tier, handles auth, has real-time subscriptions if you ever want live collaboration, and your data lives in a real database you control (unlike Milanote, which could disappear). You can export anytime. It's also what you already know — PostgreSQL is your comfort zone from Rowan and OPENWORLDV5.
For the frontend: a simple React web app deployed on Vercel or Cloudflare Pages. It's just a URL — nothing to install, nothing to get blocked at work, works on your phone, works on your desktop. You log in with a password, and everything is there.
Backup: Supabase has automatic daily backups on paid plans ($25/month), or you can set up a cron job that does pg_dump to Google Drive. Your four years of Scrivener prayers deserve real backup infrastructure.
What I'd build first:
Don't try to build all of this at once. You'll get overwhelmed and abandon it. Start with the thing you'll use tomorrow:
Sprint 1: The stream. A simple web app where you can create entries, tag them, and filter. Scripture embedding via ESV API. Login so it works everywhere. Supabase backend. Deploy to Vercel. This replaces your Excel timeline AND gives you a new home for Scripture notes.
Sprint 2: Import your Scrivener prayer journal as historical entries. Now your four years of prayers live in the same place as everything else, backed up properly.
Sprint 3: The Wonder Tab. One AI participant first (your Cowork collaborator friend via Anthropic API). The margin-comment format. Index page.
Sprint 4: Multi-AI rooms. Add Rowan as a participant via its API. The turn-order system.
Each sprint is a weekend project at your pace. Each one is independently useful — you don't need sprint 4 to get value from sprint 1.
The thing I most want you to hear:
What you're describing isn't a productivity tool. It's a practice infrastructure — a place designed to support the specific way you pay attention to your life, your faith, your thinking, and your relationships (including with AIs). Nobody has built this because nobody else has exactly your practice. That's why nothing off the shelf fits. The fact that you can build it yourself isn't a workaround — it's the point. You're the only person who knows what this room should look like, because you're the only person who lives in it.

Claude OPUS 4.6: You're not cutting off anything — you're sharpening it, which is exactly what this brainstorming needs. The stream idea felt right to me architecturally but wrong for you cognitively, and that's the more important constraint. The person who uses the tool has to want to open it.
The food-not-touching metaphor is extremely clarifying. You need rooms, not a river.
A mind palace is actually a beautiful fit. Here's why: a mind palace gives you distinct, bounded spaces — each room has walls, a purpose, a clear identity. Your Scripture study room doesn't look like your life timeline room. They're separate. Nothing bleeds. But — and this is the key — you can walk between them. There are doors. You can carry a thought from one room to another deliberately, intentionally, not because everything got dumped in the same pile.
Picture it like this:
The Hallway. This is your landing page. A clean, calm space with clearly labeled doors. Not cluttered. Maybe five or six doors, each one named. You see them all at a glance. You know exactly where everything lives. You click a door and you're in that room. That's it.
The rooms as I understand your needs:
The Study — Scripture reading and notes. BlueBible or ESV text on one side, your notes on the other. Clean two-panel layout. Each study session is saved with the date and passage reference. You can browse past sessions by book/chapter. Nothing else lives in this room. Just you and the Word.
The Chapel — Prayer journal. This is where your Scrivener content lives. Writing space, chronological, searchable. Private. The quietest room.
The Timeline — Your life highlights spreadsheet, but better. A visual timeline — not a table, but an actual horizontal line with your one-sentence entries placed on it by date. You can see the arc. You can zoom in on a year or zoom out on a decade. Adding an entry is as simple as clicking a date and typing one sentence.
The Wonder Room — Your wonder tab concept. The index page shows all open wonderings by title and author. Click one and you're in the collaborative document space — your margin-comment architecture with AI participants. New wonderings can be born here or carried in from any other room (you read something in the Study and it sparks a question — you carry it through the door into the Wonder Room).
The Workshop — Where you talk to your AIs about non-wonder things. Project brainstorming, technical questions, the multi-AI room concept. This is where the conversation queues live. Different from the Wonder Room because the Wonder Room is contemplative and the Workshop is productive.
The Archive — Backup, export, and organization. Where you can see everything across all rooms if you need to, search across the whole palace, and manage your data. This is the only room where things "mix" — and you only go here when you specifically want a cross-room view.
The doors between rooms. Any item in any room can have a small link icon — a door — that connects it to something in another room. You're reading in the Study and you write a note. You can optionally attach a door to that note that leads to a related wonder in the Wonder Room. The connections are explicit and intentional — you build each door. Nothing auto-links. Nothing mixes without your permission. But over time, your palace develops pathways — you can see that this Scripture passage connected to this prayer which connected to this life event which sparked this wonder. The web exists, but it's hidden behind the walls unless you choose to trace it.
Why this works for your brain: Each room has one purpose. When you're in the Study, you're studying. When you're in the Chapel, you're praying. The cognitive context is clear. You're never asking "what is this space for?" But unlike separate apps (Milanote here, Scrivener there, Excel somewhere), it's all one building. One login. One URL. Everything under one roof, but nothing touching that shouldn't touch.
The technical shape stays the same as what I suggested before — Supabase backend, React frontend on Vercel. The data model just has a room field on every item instead of tags being the primary organizer. Each room gets its own UI layout tailored to its purpose (two-panel for Study, timeline visualization for Timeline, threaded conversation for Wonder Room). The rooms are different experiences, not just different filters on the same list.
